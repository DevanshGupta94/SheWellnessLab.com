A Guide to Women's Health

Every stage of life has its own rhythm.

From the first changes of puberty through pregnancy, postpartum, and menopause — clear, grounded information for whatever stage you're in, and whatever questions come with it.

Life Stages

A journey with distinct chapters

Each stage brings its own physical changes and its own questions. Here's a grounded look at what tends to happen, and when to loop in a doctor.

Puberty
Roughly ages 8–16

The body's first major transition

Puberty typically begins with breast development, followed by growth spurts, body hair, and the first period (menarche), usually within two to three years. Cycles are often irregular for the first year or two as hormones settle into a pattern — this is normal, though very heavy, very painful, or absent periods by age 15–16 are worth discussing with a doctor.

Growth & first cycles
Reproductive Years
Roughly ages 15–45

Cycles, fertility & everyday health

This is the longest stage, spanning regular menstrual cycles, contraceptive decisions, and fertility planning if and when it's relevant. A typical cycle runs 21–35 days; tracking length, flow, and symptoms over a few months gives useful context for any doctor's visit. Conditions like PCOS and endometriosis, which affect a meaningful share of women, are often under-diagnosed here — persistent pain or irregular cycles are worth raising early rather than waiting.

Pregnancy
Roughly 40 weeks

Three trimesters, three different experiences

The first trimester brings the biggest hormonal shift and often nausea and fatigue; the second is typically the most comfortable, with visible growth and fetal movement; the third involves the most physical load as the body prepares for birth. Regular prenatal visits track blood pressure, growth, and shared decisions like screening tests — and it's always reasonable to call your provider with anything that feels off rather than waiting for the next appointment.

Postpartum
First 6–12 months after birth

Recovery is its own stage, not an afterthought

Physical healing — from delivery itself, shifting hormones, and (if applicable) breastfeeding — typically takes months, not weeks, and mental health checks matter just as much as physical ones. Mood changes are common in the first two weeks; persistent sadness, anxiety, or detachment beyond that window is worth telling a provider about, since postpartum depression is treatable and screened for at most follow-up visits.

Perimenopause
Typically mid-to-late 40s

The transition before the transition

Perimenopause can last several years and often shows up first as irregular cycles, sleep disruption, and hot flashes, well before periods stop altogether. Because symptoms overlap with other conditions, tracking changes over a few months helps a doctor tell perimenopause apart from, say, a thyroid issue.

Menopause & Beyond
Average age 51

A new baseline, not a decline

Menopause is officially marked by 12 consecutive months without a period. Afterward, lower estrogen affects bone density and heart health, which is why this stage often brings a shift toward bone scans and cardiovascular check-ins alongside management of ongoing symptoms like hot flashes or sleep changes.

Topics Worth Understanding

Across every stage

Some things matter at almost every point in the journey, no matter which stage you're currently in.

Mental Health

Hormonal shifts at every stage — puberty, cycles, postpartum, perimenopause — can affect mood. Persistent changes are worth naming to a provider, not pushing through alone.

Tracking & Records

A simple log of cycle length, symptoms, and changes over time turns "something feels off" into useful, specific information for a doctor's visit.

Bone & Heart Health

Estrogen protects bone density and cardiovascular health for much of life — which is why both need more active attention from perimenopause onward.

Preventive Screening

Pap smears, mammograms, and bone density scans are scheduled differently by age and history — a primary care provider can build the right calendar.

Pain Isn't "Normal"

Period pain that disrupts daily life, sex that hurts, or pelvic pain outside a cycle are common but not something to just live with — they're worth investigating.

Knowing What's Typical

"Typical" varies a lot between individuals. Understanding your own baseline makes it much easier to notice — and describe — when something genuinely changes.

None of this replaces a conversation with your own doctor.

This site is meant to help you understand your body and know what questions to ask — not to diagnose anything. If something feels persistently wrong, a healthcare provider who knows your history is always the right next step.
